# Post-Production & Editor Handoff

Captured from Adrien (Iman team — editor workflow lead) + Anouar Rahmouni (Iman team — packaging/strategy lead) on the 2026-05-13 coach call. The `youtube-scripting` skill was built by Kyle on the same team. This reference completes the scripting → post-production handoff so the operator doesn't lose value between filming and publishing.

---

## Brief structure (Adrien's template — Iman team)

Every editor handoff uses this brief structure. The template includes:

1. **Goal** — what's the video optimising for (authority, sales, brand repositioning, engagement)? What should the viewer think/feel after watching?
2. **Target** — who exactly is this for, level (beginner / intermediate / advanced), specific pain points to emphasise.
3. **Vibe** — energy level, pace, emotional tone, music direction, colour grading, motion graphic density.
4. **References** — videos, screenshots, songs, with "why" appended to each. *Always add why.*
5. **Script / structure** — final script attached or bullet points, where to tighten, where to let breathe, CTA tone, pattern interrupt locations.
6. **Production details** — audio issues, multiple angles, mistakes to cut around, moments to highlight.
7. **Branding guidelines** — fonts (name + file), lower thirds, subtitle style, logo placement, brand colours, graphic pack folder.
8. **Deliverables** — export format, thumbnail frame suggestions, social cutdowns needed.

The brief lives WITH the footage handoff to the editor.

## Music direction — operator preference, not skill default

The skill does NOT prescribe music. Operator picks based on the channel's vibe.

For Lauren specifically (locked 2026-05-13): **NO MUSIC.** Silent room tone / natural ambient only. The screen-record overlays + delivery + stat-card sound design carry the energy. Music distracts from the operator-authority tone.

Other operators may want restrained, beat-driven instrumental (Iman Gadzhi style) with swells at proof reveals and silence on identity-line moments. Specify in the brief.

## AI-on-screen style (when the video features AI tooling)

Talking-head + HEAVY screen-recording overlay of the AI agents actually working. The viewer should SEE the system live, not just hear about it.

When the creator mentions an agent or tool, the editor cuts to a real screen capture of that agent doing its job:
- DM closing in HighLevel AI Studio
- Make.com webhook firing
- Claude generating a brief or scoring a transcript
- Slack notification dropping a prep brief
- WhatsApp / second-screen prompt for a live sales coach
- CRM showing a re-engagement draft auto-populated

Reference for the AI-on-screen density: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fdN8BlQ-aYE&t=128s — Lauren's prior video where this style worked.

The principle: every claim about what the AI does should be paired with a 2–4 second cut of the AI actually doing it. Avoid abstract motion graphics in place of real screen recordings — the proof IS the recording.

---

## Motion graphics density

- **Stat cards** at every dollar/multiplier beat in the script. Specific numbers get a full-screen card or large overlay. Generic numbers (e.g. "millions of dollars") do not.
- **Framework diagrams** that appear once in the intro and get each component highlighted as the creator reaches it in the body. Useful for multi-section videos (e.g. "the 7 ai agents" / "the four 1's" / "the 5 pillars").
- **Cause labels** at the start of each new section — names the segment for the viewer.
- **Screen recording overlays** as the dominant visual element (see above).
- Avoid: text-on-screen-only sections, kinetic typography, AI-generated background imagery, flashy transitions.

---

## Pacing rules

- Average shot length **~2–3 seconds**.
- **Longer holds (5–8s)** on emotional moments — let visceral lines land before cutting.
- **Faster cuts** on stat reveals — pair with full-screen stat cards.
- **Let identity-shift lines breathe** — slowest moment of the video should be on the line that crosses the viewer's identity (e.g. *"this is the line. on one side, you hire. on the other, you build."*).
- The brief specifies "Where to let moments breathe" explicitly — list the exact lines and instruct the editor to hold them.

## Thumbnail brief — Anouar's method

**Push the agency/designer for 3 to 5 completely DIFFERENT concepts, not subtle variants.**

Anouar verbatim: *"I don't want concepts with just me on the side, and then two logos here, and one time there's fire, the second time there's a text. That's one concept, subtle changes."*

### Workflow

1. **Creator provides 3 thumbnail references in the brief.** What thumbnails work as outliers on this channel or in this niche. With "why" appended.
2. **Agency does their own research too.** Search YouTube for the topic, find what's working, factor it in.
3. **Agency delivers 3 completely different concepts minimum.** Different compositions, different visual hooks, different cause labels.
4. **Up to 5 total options** = 3 unique + 2 still-variants (add stills here and there to the 3 unique).
5. **Review rounds + feedback** to refine.

### Adopt Iman's playlist-of-references method

Iman maintains a personal playlist of thumbnails he likes. Per upload, he sends references from that playlist to his designer. Adopt the same:
- Editor + thumbnail designer keep a shared playlist of approved thumbnail references for the channel
- Reference per video to brief the agency

### Anti-patterns
- ❌ AI-generated thumbnail imagery
- ❌ Logo stacks
- ❌ Warning-tape / "ILLEGAL" / "BANNED" overlays (look like clickbait scam)
- ❌ 3 thumbnails that are 95% the same with subtle differences ("face + 2 logos, one with fire, one with text")
- ❌ Thumbnail text that just repeats the title

## Packaging-change tracking discipline (post-publish)

Every title and thumbnail change after publish gets logged:

1. **Screenshot the analytics BEFORE the change.** Views, CTR, AVD, traffic source mix, retention curve.
2. **Make the change.**
3. **Screenshot AFTER 24-48 hours.** Compare.
4. **Log in a tracking spreadsheet / Notion board** so the pattern compounds across videos.
5. **Use thumbnailtest.com properly.** If it glitches mid-test, manually revert and screenshot.

Anouar verbatim: *"I wouldn't make changes to a video before checking the analytics."* + *"It looks like it [the title change killed the momentum], from here."* (referring to a Lauren video where an untracked change tanked an upward trajectory).

Never change packaging mid-momentum without checking the retention/views chart first. If a video is on an upward curve, leave the packaging alone until it plateaus.

---

## Retention curve as the primary post-publish optimisation signal

Per Anouar: the retention curve is the main signal to study after publishing.

- **Peaks** = visuals/lines that worked. Apply more of that in next videos.
- **Dips** = where viewers leave. Cut or rework those moments in next scripts.
- **Visuals on screen typically cause retention spikes.** Editor should aim for more on-screen visual density per the AI-on-screen principle.
- This isn't just for the current video — it's how the channel learns video-over-video.

Editor + creator should jointly review the retention curve a week after publish and document patterns in a shared doc.

## AVD targets by video length

| Video length | AVD target |
|---|---|
| 10 min | 3–4 min AVD (YouTube universal average) |
| 12–15 min | 4 min AVD |
| 20+ min | proportionally — aim 25–35% retention |

Don't artificially extend video length to "improve AVD." YouTube uses % viewed, not duration. A 90-min video with 10 min AVD is worse than a 15-min video with 4 min AVD.

## Social cutdowns from the master video

Standard cutdown set:

| Platform | Length | Content |
|---|---|---|
| **LinkedIn** | 1–2 min | Hook + ONE strongest section + CTA |
| **Instagram Reels / TikTok** | 60–90s | Vertical 9:16, hook + sequence tease + "watch full on YouTube" |
| **X / Twitter** | 45–60s | Punchy hook + identity line + link |

Each cutdown gets its own edit pass — don't just crop the master.

---

## Subtitle / lower-third style (operator choice)

Set in the brief per channel. Common patterns:
- Lowercase if the creator's brand voice is lowercase (LinkedIn-style)
- Standard caps for sophisticated/B2B audiences
- White text + subtle drop shadow for readability
- Bottom-centred, 2 lines max
- Native YouTube auto-captions are NOT enough — produce baked-in or proper SRT

---

## Master export format

- **4K MP4, H.264, 16:9 (3840×2160)** for YouTube master
- 1080p backup
- Audio: stereo 48kHz minimum
- Loudness target: -14 LUFS integrated (YouTube standard)
